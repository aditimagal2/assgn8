Ameriprise Website Navigation

Redesigning the mobile navigation experience in 4 weeks without changing the existing navigation structure of the website.

Ameriprise (March 2019- April 2019) Year | Collaboration with product owners, developers, content writers, design strategists and user researchers.

While working at a financial advisory firm (Ameriprise) as a product designer I had the opportunity to work on redesigning Ameriprise’s mobile navigation.

The restrictions placed on new designs and the large number of people involved in the project made it challenging.

Ameriprise’s website structure had three levels: section front, topic and article. The business required all pages to be displayed in the navigation, which made it hard when each section front acted as a label and page by itself.

My comparative analysis with competitors and other industries revealed that other websites used touch as a way to get to different levels. For example, a single tap would go to a section front, but holding down on a label would reveal a dropdown menu taking you to more topics.

Knowing Ameriprise’s business clientele my assumption was that it would be hard for our users to distinguish between tapping and holding down labels without visual cues. User testing was necessary to confirm or deny my hypothesis.

Talking to my Design strategist who conducted a thorough analysis of our existing navigation page I was able to identify the labels and section fronts that are used most by users. Together we were also able to identify some navigation pieces that were unnecessary or repetitive. For example, the old navigation had the Ameriprise logo and “home” label placed next to each other which both lead to the landing page.

I designed my first two prototype in sketch and invision. The two ideas for mobile navigation:

1. Drilldown

2. Drilldown and accordion

I worked with the user researcher who conducted three rounds of testing to investigate different interactions on the two mockups.

A remote click test that was sent to 109 employee-prospects.

A guerrilla test with 15 employees

A remote click test sent to 89 clients

Participants had trouble drilling down into section front pages when there were multiple options to choose from.

Testing the prototypes confirmed my hypothesis that having two hit areas on the same row of navigation within a mobile menu will increase error.

The best solution was the one where the first level was a drill down that then led to a drop-down accordion:

Working with content and user research our final solution made it easier to navigate multiple pages on Ameriprise’s website.

The new navigation is currently being built by the public site agile team.
